Teach a Robot to Navigate Your Home

Embrained is an open-source platform for training and deploying neural navigation models on real robotic hardware — using your own laptop GPU.

Our robot, the Plexus, is a camera on wheels that streams live video to your computer over WiFi. You drive it around to collect training data, run Python scripts to train perception and navigation models, then hand control back to the AI and watch what it learned. Today, the Cognitive Engine handles the full loop: data collection, VAE training, CQL policy learning, and real-time autonomous deployment.

Everything runs locally on your own laptop GPU. No cloud. No subscription. No internet required. Your camera data never leaves your machine.

What Works Today

The Cognitive Engine handles the hardest part of the problem: teaching a robot to navigate reliably using only a camera and cheap motors, with no encoders, no LiDAR, and no odometry. A VQ-VAE compresses camera frames into a learned spatial manifold. A CQL policy selects actions to drive toward a goal. The robot navigates autonomously in real homes.

A fully autonomous Plexus navigating to a goal using a learned VQ-VAE/CQL policy.

Out of the box, you get the full pipeline: teleop data collection, VAE training, CQL policy learning, Markov exploration, Patrol mode, a live dashboard with latent space visualization, and a scoreboard for evaluating model performance. All running locally on your GPU.

Where We're Going

The navigation engine is the foundation. The next layer we're building is visual semantic navigation — making the robot respond to natural language commands:

"Is there laundry on the living room floor?"
"Go see if I left the front door open."
"Find my keys."

This will require three layers running at different speeds on your PC — the robot stays a thin sensor/actuator relay:

Navigation Engine ✓ Live today

10Hz closed-loop control

The Cognitive Engine — the core that already works. A VQ-VAE compresses camera frames into a learned spatial manifold and a CQL policy selects actions to drive toward a goal. This is the hard part, and it's done.

Scene Oracle — Coming soon

On-demand (~1–3 seconds per query)

A local Vision-Language Model (Moondream or Gemma 3) will run on your GPU alongside the navigation engine. The camera frame is already on your PC from the WiFi stream — the VLM will simply answer questions about what it sees. No cloud API, no per-query cost.

Language Planner — Coming soon

Runs once per command

Will decompose natural language requests into navigation goals and observation tasks. "Go check the front door" becomes: navigate to "front door" → observe "is it open?" → report answer.

The architecture for this is already in place. Because everything runs on the user's PC, adding a VLM is just another GPU process alongside the Cognitive Engine — no hardware changes, no cloud dependency.

Why Nobody Else Can Do This

An affordable robot that learns to navigate real homes using only a camera — with a full training pipeline included out of the box. That doesn't exist yet.

Consumer Robots (Loona, Enabot)

Cloud-dependent, no learned navigation, gets stuck in clutter, can't navigate to named locations. $400+.

Educational Kits (PiCar-X, Raspbot)

Open hardware, but ships with nothing beyond line-following scripts. A bag of parts and a screwdriver. No neural network integration, no training pipeline, no path to real AI. ~$220.

Research Platforms (TurtleBot 4, LIMO)

Capable open ROS 2 platforms with LiDAR and depth cameras. But $1,200–$3,700+, require expert setup, and offer no integrated train-from-your-home pipeline.

A Plexus buyer gets an assembled robot, a one-line setup script, and immediate access to the full Cognitive Engine — teleop data collection, VAE training, CQL policy learning, Markov exploration, Patrol mode, a live dashboard with latent space visualization, and a scoreboard. When we add the VLM layer, they'll also get a robot that can answer questions about what it sees — all running locally.

Your Data Stays Home

Every image your robot captures stays on your local network. Training runs on your GPU. Nothing is uploaded to our servers.

When you're ready to contribute to the community, you share only the trained model weights — compact mathematical tensors that contain no recognizable imagery of your home. This privacy-preserving approach lets us build increasingly capable models across many environments without any single user's raw data ever leaving their machine.
