Embrained is an open-source platform for visual semantic navigation — teaching a real robot to navigate your home by name, understand what it sees, and respond to natural language requests. The robot drives to locations it has learned, looks at the scene through its camera, and reports back in plain language.

Everything runs locally on your own laptop GPU. No cloud. No subscription. No internet required. Your camera data never leaves your machine.

Three Layers, One Robot

Visual semantic navigation requires three capabilities running at completely different speeds. Our disaggregated intelligence architecture runs all three on your PC — the robot is a thin sensor/actuator relay over WiFi.

Language Planner

Runs once per command (~2–5 seconds)

Decomposes a natural language request into a task sequence. "Go see if I left the front door open" becomes: navigate to "front door" → observe "is it open?" → report answer. For v1 this uses pattern matching; future versions can use the same local VLM for richer decomposition.

Navigation Engine

Runs at 10Hz, continuous closed-loop control

This is the Cognitive Engine — the core that already works. A VQ-VAE compresses camera frames into a learned spatial manifold. A CQL policy selects actions to drive toward a goal. Users label manifold regions with semantic names ("kitchen", "front door"), and the planner maps location names to goal latents. The existing navigation loop handles the rest.

Scene Oracle

Runs on-demand (~1–3 seconds per query)

A local Vision-Language Model (Moondream or Gemma 3) running on your GPU. It receives the current camera frame — already on your PC from the WiFi stream — and answers questions about what it sees. No cloud API, no per-query cost. The VLM doesn't touch the navigation loop; it fires once when the robot arrives at its destination.

The Hard Part Is Already Done

The VLM and language planner are commodity technology — pip install moondream, pass it a frame, get an answer. What's hard is the navigation: teaching a robot to reliably drive from point A to point B in a real home, using only a camera and cheap motors, with no encoders, no LiDAR, and no odometry.

That's what the Cognitive Engine does, and it already works. You can see it in action below — a fully autonomous Plexus navigating to a goal using a learned discrete VQ-VAE/CQL policy.

Adding scene understanding on top — "is the door open?" — is furniture. The navigation is the load-bearing wall. Everything else plugs into infrastructure that's already built.

Why Nobody Else Can Do This

A $199 robot that navigates to named locations using a learned policy and answers natural language questions about what it sees — running entirely on the user's own GPU with zero cloud dependency. That doesn't exist yet.

Consumer Robots (Loona, Enabot)

Cloud-dependent, no learned navigation, gets stuck in clutter, can't navigate to named locations. $400+.

Educational Kits (PiCar-X, Raspbot)

Open hardware, but ships with nothing beyond line-following scripts. A bag of parts and a screwdriver. No neural network integration, no training pipeline, no path to real AI. ~$220.

Research Platforms (TurtleBot 4, LIMO)

Fully capable ROS 2 platforms with LiDAR and depth cameras. But $1,200–$3,700 and no integrated VLM scene understanding.

A Plexus buyer gets an assembled robot, a one-line setup script, and immediate access to the full Cognitive Engine — teleop data collection, VAE training, CQL policy learning, Markov exploration, Patrol mode, a live dashboard with latent space visualization, and a scoreboard. When we add the VLM layer, they'll also get a robot that can answer questions about what it sees — all running locally.

Your Data Stays Home

Every image your robot captures stays on your local network. Training runs on your GPU. VLM inference runs on your GPU. Nothing is uploaded to our servers — not your camera frames, not your navigation data, not your questions.

When you're ready to contribute to the community, you share only the trained model weights — compact mathematical tensors that contain no recognizable imagery of your home. This privacy-preserving approach lets us build increasingly capable models across many environments without any single user's raw data ever leaving their machine.
