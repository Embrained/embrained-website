Train Your Own AI Robot at Home

Embrained makes affordable robots and open-source software that let you collect data, train neural networks, and watch your AI models drive a real robot around your living room — all using your own laptop GPU.

Our robot, the Plexus, is a camera on wheels that streams live video to your computer over WiFi. You drive it around to collect training data, run Python scripts to train perception and navigation models, then hand control back to the AI and see what it learned. The entire loop — data collection, training, and real-time inference — runs locally on your machine. No cloud. No subscription. No expensive hardware.

We are building this in public because visual navigation — teaching a robot to go somewhere using only what it sees — is one of the hardest unsolved problems in AI, and we believe the best way to crack it is together.

Why This Matters

Imagine telling a robot "go to the TV, or go to the kitchen" and having it navigate there on its own, using nothing but a camera. No lidar, no pre-built map, no thousand-dollar GPU bolted to its back. Just vision — the way animals do it.

That capability doesn't exist yet. State-of-the-art navigation robots cost thousands of dollars, require onboard GPUs, and are trained in controlled labs. Embrained takes the opposite approach: we use an affordable robot with no onboard compute, stream its camera feed to your laptop, and let you train the brain yourself. The result is an open research platform where anyone with a mid-range GPU can run real embodied AI experiments at home.

The Science Behind the Platform

We are solving one of the hardest problems in edge robotics: teaching a machine to navigate using only vision over a high-latency WiFi connection, without an onboard GPU. To keep the Plexus affordable and indefinite in lifespan, we offload all neural compute to your laptop. However, this introduces an unavoidable half-second delay between emitting a motor command and seeing its physical effect captured by the camera.

Current Milestone: Discrete Goal Navigation
Our first major breakthrough overcomes this latency by treating movement as a turn-based game (a discrete Markov Decision Process). A Variational Autoencoder (VAE) compresses stationary, blur-free camera frames into a compact "mental map", and a Conservative Q-Learning (CQL) policy selects the single best action to reach a goal. You can see this in action below: the robot successfully learns to navigate to specific targets using only its camera.

A fully autonomous Plexus navigating to a goal using a learned discrete VQ-VAE/CQL policy.

The Next Step: Continuous Action Chunking
While mathematically elegant, the discrete approach results in clumsy, stop-start movement. To achieve the fluid, continuous navigation that humans use when teleoperating the robot, we are currently transitioning to a Continuous Action Chunking architecture. Instead of single moves, the policy learns to predict sequences of fluid motion primitives, overlapping them temporally to hide the network latency and keep the robot moving gracefully. Eventually, we will scale up to forward models and VLAs—but always under our core constraint: ensuring everything can be trained remotely on local hardware without ever uploading raw visual data.

By joining the Beta Pilot, you aren't just buying a toy—you are joining a distributed lab. You'll help us collect the continuous trajectory datasets required to train the next generation of fluid neural navigators.

Your Data Stays Home

Every image your robot captures stays on your local network. Training runs on your GPU. Nothing is uploaded to our servers.

When you're ready to contribute to the community, you share only the trained model weights — compact mathematical tensors that contain no recognizable imagery of your home. This privacy-preserving approach lets us build increasingly capable "master models" across many environments without any single user's raw data ever leaving their machine.
