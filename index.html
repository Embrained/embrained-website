Train Your Own AI Robot at Home

Embrained makes affordable robots and open-source software that let you collect data, train neural networks, and watch your AI models drive a real robot around your living room — all using your own laptop GPU.

Our robot, the Plexus, is a camera on wheels that streams live video to your computer over WiFi. You drive it around to collect training data, run Python scripts to train perception and navigation models, then hand control back to the AI and see what it learned. The entire loop — data collection, training, and real-time inference — runs locally on your machine. No cloud. No subscription. No expensive hardware.

We are building this in public because visual navigation — teaching a robot to go somewhere using only what it sees — is one of the hardest unsolved problems in AI, and we believe the best way to crack it is together.

Why This Matters

Imagine telling a robot "go to the kitchen" and having it navigate there on its own, using nothing but a camera. No lidar, no pre-built map, no thousand-dollar GPU bolted to its back. Just vision — the way animals do it.

That capability doesn't exist yet. State-of-the-art navigation robots cost thousands of dollars, require onboard GPUs, and are trained in controlled labs. Embrained takes the opposite approach: we use an affordable robot with no onboard compute, stream its camera feed to your laptop, and let you train the brain yourself. The result is an open research platform where anyone with a mid-range GPU can run real embodied AI experiments at home.

The Science Behind the Platform

Visual navigation sits at the intersection of neuroscience, robotics, and machine learning. At its core, the system works like this: a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) compresses each camera frame into a compact mathematical representation — a "mental map" of what the robot has seen. A Conservative Q-Learning (CQL) policy then learns which motor actions move the robot closer to a target location in that map.

The deeper implications are profound. The same mathematical framework that lets a small robot learn to find your sofa connects directly to theories of biological navigation and intelligence. If you're a neuroscience researcher, a robotics student, or simply curious about how brains might work, the Plexus gives you a physical system to experiment with these ideas directly.

Your Data Stays Home

Every image your robot captures stays on your local network. Training runs on your GPU. Nothing is uploaded to our servers.

When you're ready to contribute to the community, you share only the trained model weights — compact mathematical tensors that contain no recognizable imagery of your home. This privacy-preserving approach lets us build increasingly capable "master models" across many environments without any single user's raw data ever leaving their machine.
