Hardware & Software

Nobody is buying Plexus for the hardware. They're buying it because when they open the box, they can train a neural navigation model in 5 minutes.

The Plexus is a camera on wheels — deliberately simple, deliberately cheap. The real product is the Cognitive Engine: the full-stack software platform that turns camera frames into learned navigation behavior, running entirely on your own laptop GPU.

The Plexus Robot

Plexus is a small, two-wheeled robot built from standard, replaceable parts: an ESP32-S3 microcontroller with a camera, two TT gearbox motors, lithium-ion batteries, and a custom PCB that handles power management and motor control. It connects to your laptop over WiFi and streams live camera frames at up to 10 fps.

There is no onboard GPU, no lidar, and no depth sensor. This is deliberate. By keeping the hardware simple and cheap, we ensure that your laptop is the brain. The robot never becomes obsolete — when better models come along, you just update your software. And because every Plexus shares the exact same physical form factor (camera height, field of view, gear ratios), models trained by one user can transfer to another user's robot.

The Plexus uses imprecise, non-linear hobby motors without wheel encoders — the kind that slip on carpet and drift on hard floors. This is a feature, not a bug. Navigation models that learn to succeed despite this noise are far more robust than models trained on precision hardware in a lab.

What's in the Box

  • XIAO ESP32-S3 Sense — Microcontroller with OV2640 camera module
  • Plexus Beta Board — Custom PCB handling motor drivers, power regulation, and sensor inputs
  • 2× TT Gearbox Motors — Differential drive with rubber wheels
  • Laser-Cut Acrylic Chassis — Three-plate sandwich design
  • Ball Caster & Ultrasonic Sensor — Rear support and forward obstacle detection

Note: 18650 Li-Ion batteries are not included due to shipping restrictions. You will need to purchase two batteries separately.

Why Identical Hardware Matters

One of the biggest bottlenecks in robotics research is data fragmentation: every lab uses different robots, so datasets can't be combined. The Plexus solves this by design. Every unit has the same camera, the same motors, and the same physical dimensions.

This means data collected by one user is structurally compatible with data from any other user. Over time, the community can build a shared corpus of navigation data spanning hundreds of distinct living rooms, kitchens, and offices — without anyone uploading a single raw image.

Instead of sharing images, users share only the mathematical weights of their locally-trained models. These anonymous tensors are averaged to produce increasingly capable "master models" that have effectively learned from many environments while keeping every user's visual data private. This is privacy-preserving federated learning applied to physical robots.

The Cognitive Engine

The Embrained app is a browser-based dashboard that runs locally on your machine. It connects to your Plexus over WiFi and gives you everything you need in one interface: live camera feed, manual driving controls, data recording, model training, autonomous deployment, and real-time latent space visualization.

Drive & Record

Use WASD keys to drive the robot through your space. The app automatically logs camera frames, motor commands, and sensor data into structured datasets. Built-in autonomous exploration modes can also generate training data without manual input.

Train

Run Python scripts to train your models locally:

  • Perception (VQ-VAE) — Learn a compressed visual representation of your environment
  • Navigation (CQL) — Learn which actions move the robot toward a goal location
  • Coming soon: Scene Understanding (VLM) — A local Vision-Language Model that answers questions about what the robot sees

All training and inference runs locally on your GPU. A mid-range laptop (4-8GB VRAM) is sufficient.

Deploy & Evaluate

Load your trained model and hand control to the AI. The dashboard shows the model's latent space position, its goal, and the motor commands it's choosing — in real time. Built-in evaluation tools automatically score navigation success across repeated trials.

Why Plexus?

You cannot buy a sleek, affordable, natively open-source robot designed to pipe video directly into a neural network. The closest comparisons are PiCar-X (~$220) and Yahboom Raspbot. But buying one gets you a bag of parts, a screwdriver, and a link to some OpenCV line-following scripts.

A Plexus buyer gets: an assembled robot, a one-line setup script, and immediate access to the full Cognitive Engine — teleop data collection, VAE training, CQL policy learning, Markov exploration, Patrol mode, a live dashboard with latent space visualization, and a scoreboard.

Plexus hardware is comparable to a PiCar-X or Raspbot in raw capability — cheap motors, a single camera, no encoders, no LiDAR. And that's fine, because the hardware is a means to an end. The end is a robot that understands your home — and soon, one that responds to what you ask it.

Where We're Going

The navigation engine is the foundation. The next layer is visual semantic navigation — teaching the robot to respond to natural language commands like "Is there laundry on the living room floor?" or "Go see if I left the front door open."

This requires a local Vision-Language Model running on your GPU alongside the Cognitive Engine. The robot navigates to a named location using its learned policy, observes the scene through its camera, and the VLM interprets what it sees — all without touching the cloud.

The navigation — the hard part — is already done. The language understanding is commodity technology that plugs into the infrastructure we've already built.
